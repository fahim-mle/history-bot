The Fall of Rome

The Western Roman Empire officially fell in 476 AD when the Germanic chieftain Odoacer deposed the last Roman emperor, Romulus Augustulus. This event is traditionally used to mark the end of ancient history and the beginning of the Middle Ages in Europe.

1

The decline of Rome was a gradual process spanning centuries. Historians have debated its causes extensively. Edward Gibbon in his famous work pointed to the rise of Christianity and the transformation of Roman civic values. Others emphasize military overextension, economic troubles, and the pressure of migrating peoples along the frontiers.

2

Constantine the Great moved the imperial capital to Constantinople in 330 AD, splitting the empire's administrative centre eastward. The Eastern Roman Empire, which we call the Byzantine Empire, would survive for nearly a thousand more years until the Ottoman conquest of Constantinople in 1453.

The internal instability of the third century saw dozens of emperors rise and fall in rapid succession. The so-called Crisis of the Third Century (235–284 AD) brought plague, invasion, and economic collapse simultaneously. Diocletian's reforms stabilized the empire temporarily, but the structural weaknesses remained.

3

By the late fourth century, Visigoths and other Germanic peoples had been settled within Roman borders as foederati — allied troops who fought under Roman command. When Roman authorities mistreated the Visigoths, they revolted, defeating and killing Emperor Valens at the Battle of Adrianople in 378 AD. This battle demonstrated that the empire could no longer guarantee its own borders.

The sack of Rome by the Visigoths under Alaric in 410 AD shocked the Roman world. Augustine of Hippo began writing The City of God partly in response to pagan accusations that Christianity had weakened Rome's traditional defenses.
